CHAPTER ONE

THE BOY WHO SURVIVED

Stan Edgar, of number four, Privet Drive, was proud to say that his household was normal in every measurable way. Respectable, quiet, insured, efficiently managed, and entirely free of the sort of public mess that followed capes, masks, impact craters, congressional hearings, classified evacuations, and people who used words like destiny when they meant property damage.

Stan liked a closed file, a signed nondisclosure agreement, a polished shoe, and a morning paper folded exactly along the crease. He did not like surprises. He did not like loose ends. He especially did not like enhanced people unless they were properly contracted, legally contained, publicly marketable, and obedient to the interests of people who understood balance sheets.

Madelyn Stillwell, his wife, shared most of these opinions, though she expressed them with a brighter smile and sharper teeth. She was blonde, immaculate, and very good at making a room feel warmer or colder depending on what she wanted from the people in it. She kept the house spotless, the curtains tasteful, and the neighbors at a distance just close enough to admire her family and not close enough to ask questions.

The Edgars-Stillwells had a son called A-Train, though on official forms he had a longer, more respectable name that Madelyn only used when speaking to school administrators and private coaches. In their opinion, there was no finer boy in the world. He was fast for his age, loud for any age, and already possessed the remarkable ability to make adults laugh indulgently when he broke things.

Number four was therefore a house of achievement. It had trimmed hedges, a clean drive, a security system Stan insisted was merely prudent, and a front door painted a color described in the catalogue as Heritage Black. Nobody passing by would have guessed that the people inside had a secret.

They did.

The secret had a name the household did not use.

Daphne Campbell.

Madelyn's sister had not been mentioned properly in years. If neighbors asked whether Madelyn had family, Madelyn became vague in a way that made the questioner feel they had committed some social error. Daphne had been difficult, Madelyn said, if she said anything at all. Idealistic. Reckless. Drawn to dangerous people. Mixed up in circles no sensible woman should approach. Her husband, Hugh Campbell Sr., had been no better: kind, ordinary-looking, and therefore, in Stan's private view, even more dangerous, because dangerous people who looked dangerous were at least useful enough to identify.

The Campbells had also had a baby.

Stan and Madelyn knew this, though they had never visited. They had seen one photograph, forwarded years ago and deleted within minutes from Madelyn's personal account. The child had pale skin, soft hair, and a wrinkled expression of offended confusion. Daphne had written beneath the picture: Hughie says hello.

Madelyn had cried after reading it, then denied crying when Stan found her in the kitchen. Since then, the Campbells had not been discussed.

This suited Stan perfectly. He believed that a person could keep the world orderly by refusing to dignify disorder with attention. Costumed celebrity rescues on television were one thing. Product placement was one thing. Controlled hero appearances at sponsored memorials were one thing. But the world underneath all that—the classified one, the one that stole relatives, buried scandals, erased streets from maps, and sent people in dark coats to stand at hospital doors—was not welcome at number four.

On the gray morning when everything changed, there was nothing about the weather to suggest that classified agencies, alien intelligence channels, and enhanced operatives across Britain were losing their collective composure.

Stan rose at six-thirty, checked his messages, read three market summaries, rejected a tie for being too memorable, and selected a plain dark one instead. Madelyn took a call from a school liaison while spooning breakfast into A-Train, who slapped at the spoon and declared that he wanted chocolate cereal, not eggs.

"We discussed this," Madelyn said in the singsong voice she used when she was not prepared to be defied.

A-Train screamed.

"Strong lungs," Stan observed from behind the financial pages.

Madelyn smiled. "Leadership presence."

At eight-fifteen, Stan collected his briefcase. He kissed Madelyn on the cheek, bent toward A-Train, missed because A-Train was kicking the leg of his chair, and straightened with the faint satisfaction of a man whose son was already difficult for other people.

"Try not to bankrupt the house before lunch," he said.

A-Train threw a piece of egg at the wall.

"Gifted," Madelyn said.

Stan stepped outside into the cool morning. Privet Drive looked as it ought to look: identical houses, washed cars, lawns cut to an acceptable height, no crowds, no smoke, no sirens. He locked the front door and started down the path.

That was when he saw the drone.

It was small, matte black, and humming above the corner sign. Not a delivery unit. Not one of the council survey models. Its body had no visible manufacturer mark, and its lenses rotated independently with a soft mechanical tick. It hovered for three seconds over the words Privet Drive, then angled toward Stan as if considering him.

Stan stopped.

The drone dipped once, almost like a nod, then shot upward so quickly it vanished into the cloud cover.

For a moment Stan did not move. Then he looked up and down the street. Mrs. Crowley from number seven was pruning roses. A man in exercise clothes jogged past with a dog. No one seemed to have noticed anything.

A trick of perspective, Stan decided. Some hobbyist nonsense. Children with money and no discipline.

He got into his car and pulled out of the drive.

By the time he reached the main road, he had almost convinced himself the drone had been ordinary. By the time he reached the edge of town, ordinary had become harder to maintain.

There were people standing near the bus shelter.

Not commuters. Commuters looked at watches, phones, and the middle distance with resentment. These people stood in a loose knot, speaking softly and glancing at the sky. One wore a long gray coat despite the mild weather. Another had silver markings along her neck that vanished under her collar. A third held a helmet under one arm, its visor glowing faintly blue.

Stan slowed without meaning to.

One of them said something that made the others turn sharply. Stan could not hear the words over the traffic, but he saw a reaction pass through them: shock, then relief, then something dangerously close to joy.

He accelerated.

At the next junction, there were more of them. A man in a courier uniform with no company logo. Two women in dark tactical jackets sharing a tablet that projected light an inch above its surface. An elderly gentleman in a perfectly tailored suit whose shoes did not touch the pavement.

Stan gripped the steering wheel.

He disliked enhanced people in public when they were supposed to be at launches, hearings, rescues, trials, or carefully managed charity events. He disliked them even more when they appeared in clusters, whispering. Whispering meant rumors. Rumors meant instability. Instability meant loss.

He was waiting at a red light when a phrase drifted in through the cracked car window.

"...Campbells, they said. Both of them."

Stan's fingers tightened.

The light changed. A horn sounded behind him. He drove.

A mile later, as the road narrowed near the business district, another phrase followed him through the morning air from a group gathered outside a closed café.

"...Homelander's gone."

Stan nearly mounted the curb.

He corrected hard, swore under his breath, and continued toward the office with his jaw locked.

Homelander.

That name did not belong on suburban roads. It did not belong outside secure rooms, sealed court records, emergency briefings, or the kind of encrypted files that had a way of deleting themselves if unauthorized people opened them. It certainly did not belong in the mouths of people standing beside a café before nine in the morning.

Stan had met men who claimed they feared nothing. He considered them amateurs. Fear was a useful instrument if one knew where to place it. Homelander was one of the few subjects Stan preferred not to handle at all.

At work, he attempted to bury himself in normality.

He was the senior executive of a firm that manufactured precision industrial components, a description boring enough to calm him on difficult days. The factory floor ran on schedules. The contracts had numbers. The people under him feared him in predictable ways. Nothing flew unless it was shipped.

He entered the building, nodded once at reception, and took the lift to the ninth floor. His office had a view, but Stan kept his desk angled away from the windows. Views encouraged reflection, and reflection encouraged inefficiency.

By nine-thirty he had criticized a logistics memo, rejected two budget proposals, and made a procurement manager audibly regret being alive. By ten, he felt steadier.

Then his assistant knocked.

"What?" Stan said.

She opened the door just enough to show half her face. "Sir, there are reports of unauthorized aerial traffic over the city."

"If it interferes with shipping, send it to compliance."

"It isn't commercial traffic."

Stan looked up.

She swallowed. "People are posting videos. Drones. Birds. Something that looks like a man without a flight rig over Canary Wharf. The feeds keep disappearing."

"Then perhaps they weren't important."

"Yes, sir."

She withdrew.

Stan sat very still.

Feeds disappearing meant someone with reach. Someone with reach meant an agency, a contractor, or something worse. Stan opened a private news terminal and scanned headlines. Nothing prominent. A transport delay. A ministerial denial. Weather. Football. A celebrity divorce. The surface of the world remained smooth.

Beneath it, he could feel the machinery grinding.

At lunchtime, he left the office to buy coffee he did not want. He told himself walking would clear his head. He told himself many things with convincing precision.

Outside, the city had not improved.

Near the entrance to a bakery stood a group of people who would have been invisible to most passersby because most passersby had trained themselves not to notice what frightened them. Stan noticed.

One had the stillness of a professional bodyguard and the eyes of a predator. One wore a scarf wrapped too high around her throat, though the wind lifted it enough for Stan to glimpse ridged blue skin. A broad-shouldered man leaned on a cane that clicked against the pavement with the weight of something far heavier than wood.

They were speaking in low tones.

Stan moved past them, coffee in hand.

"...Daphne Campbell," the blue-skinned woman said.

He stopped.

The broad man answered, "And Hugh. Both dead, that's confirmed."

"The baby?"

A pause.

"Alive."

Stan's coffee cup bent in his hand.

The lid popped loose. Hot coffee splashed his fingers. He did not react. He stood with his back half turned, every part of him listening.

"Alive?" the woman whispered.

"Not just alive. They say the strike rebounded. They say Homelander vanished after."

"That's impossible."

"Tell that to the GDA. Mallory's moving tonight."

Stan walked away before he heard more.

He returned to his office, shut the door, and did not shout at anyone for twenty-three minutes, which caused concern across the ninth floor.

Daphne dead. Hugh dead. The baby alive.

Homelander vanished.

Mallory moving.

The names circled one another in his head like knives.

He picked up the telephone twice to call Madelyn and put it down twice. If he said Daphne's name, Madelyn would ask questions. If Madelyn asked questions, he would have to decide how much he knew and how much he could admit to knowing. If he admitted anything, then the day would become real.

Better to wait. Better to gather information. Better to let the world reveal the shape of the threat before acknowledging it.

At five o'clock he left the office with the stiff, controlled walk of a man refusing to hurry. On the pavement outside, a small woman in a violet coat collided with him so lightly that he should not have staggered.

"Watch where—"

She looked up at him.

Her eyes were not human. Not entirely. Their irises held thin rings of gold moving in opposite directions.

Stan froze.

The woman smiled with open, inappropriate delight. "Oh, don't scowl today of all days. He's gone. The butcher of half the sky has gone. Even the blind little surface people should feel the air is cleaner."

Stan's mouth tightened. "Excuse me?"

But she had already moved on, humming, her shoes clicking too quickly down the street.

Surface people.

Stan stood among the evening crowd, humiliated by the fact that his pulse had quickened.

He drove home without turning on the radio.

Privet Drive appeared exactly as he had left it. That was its finest quality. The lawns had not shifted. The windows reflected the dimming sky. No one in armor waited by the hedge. No silent aircraft hung over the roof.

Still, as Stan pulled into the drive, he saw something perched on the streetlamp opposite number four.

At first he thought it was another drone.

Then it moved.

A woman sat atop the lamp as casually as if it were a park bench, one boot braced against the metal post, a dark cloak or coat falling around her. She was too far away for him to make out her face, but when she turned her head toward him he felt, absurdly, that she had been watching him all day.

Stan got out of the car.

The figure was gone.

He stared at the empty lamp.

"Ridiculous," he said aloud.

Mrs. Crowley from number seven looked up from dragging her bin to the curb.

"Sorry?" she called.

"Nothing," Stan said.

He entered the house and locked the door behind him.

Madelyn was in the kitchen, bright and composed, cutting vegetables with unnecessary force. A-Train had apparently spent the afternoon overturning a toy box, refusing to nap, and discovering that markers worked on wallpaper. Madelyn reported all this as if describing the early years of a head of state.

Stan listened. He nodded in the correct places. He washed his hands for longer than necessary where the coffee had burned him.

At dinner, A-Train banged his spoon and demanded dessert before finishing anything else.

"Not until you eat," Madelyn said.

"I want it now."

"Ambition," Stan murmured.

Madelyn smiled faintly. "Your father means patience."

"Now!"

The spoon hit the floor.

Stan barely heard it.

After A-Train had been put to bed, which required two stories, three threats, and one negotiated promise of a new pair of training shoes, Stan and Madelyn sat in the living room. Madelyn held a glass of white wine. Stan held nothing. The television murmured through the evening news.

He intended not to speak.

He lasted until the final segment.

"And finally," said the newsreader, wearing the strained expression of a man whose producer had forced him to end on nonsense, "viewers across the country have reported unusual lights in the sky throughout the day. The Ministry of Defence has described the sightings as a combination of meteor activity and private drone misuse. Similar reports of unidentified low-altitude aircraft over London have been dismissed as internet exaggeration."

The screen cut to a weather presenter standing before a map of Britain decorated with cheerful cloud icons.

"Well, if meteors are responsible, they haven't told me. What I can promise is rain over most of the southeast by morning, with heavy cloud moving—"

Stan turned the television off.

Madelyn looked at him. "I was watching that."

"No, you weren't."

She studied him over the rim of her glass. "Something happened."

"No."

"Stan."

He disliked the way she said his name when she wanted truth. It was inconveniently intimate.

He sat back. "Have you heard from your sister recently?"

Madelyn's face changed.

It was only for a second. The polished calm cracked, and beneath it was anger, fear, and something like grief, quickly buried.

"No," she said. "Why would you ask that?"

"No reason."

"That is not a question you ask for no reason."

He glanced toward the hallway, where A-Train's room was silent.

Madelyn set down her glass. "What have you heard?"

"Rumors."

"About Daphne?"

"Possibly."

Madelyn rose. "I don't want to discuss her."

"That has been our position for years."

"Then maintain it."

"There were people in town," Stan said. "Not ordinary people."

"There are always people in town."

"They were saying the Campbells' names."

Madelyn went very still.

"And Homelander's," Stan added.

The silence that followed seemed to press against the windows.

Madelyn sat down slowly. "Don't say that name in this house."

"That name is being said on the street."

"By whom?"

"Operatives. Contractors. Things pretending to be citizens."

"What were they saying?"

Stan considered lying. But Madelyn had always been better than most people at detecting the shape of an omission.

"They said Daphne and Hugh are dead."

Madelyn closed her eyes.

For several seconds she did not speak. Stan watched her expression carefully. He saw the sister in her grief before the executive smothered it.

"The child?" she asked.

He hesitated.

Her eyes opened. "Stan."

"Alive, according to what I heard."

Madelyn pressed one hand to her mouth.

"It may be false," Stan said. "Rumor tends to breed during classified transitions."

"Alive," she repeated softly.

"And supposedly connected to whatever happened to Homelander."

The grief vanished behind alarm. "Connected how?"

"Unclear."

"Stan."

"Unclear," he repeated more sharply. "The phrase I heard was that the strike rebounded."

Madelyn stood again, walked to the window, and pulled the curtain aside by less than an inch. The street beyond was quiet and orange under the lamps.

"They'll come," she said.

"Who?"

"You know who."

He did.

The agencies. The old soldiers. The moralists. The people who believed blood created obligation. Perhaps Mallory herself, if the rumors were true.

"We are not suitable," Madelyn said.

"No."

"We cannot have that world here."

"No."

"We have A-Train."

"I am aware."

"We have built a life."

"I am aware."

She turned from the window. "Then if anyone comes, we are not home."

Stan almost smiled. "A difficult position to maintain while standing in the living room."

"I am serious."

"So am I."

A faint sound came from outside.

Both of them stopped.

It might have been wind. It might have been the settling of pipes. It might have been something moving lightly over the roof.

Stan went to the window and looked out.

Nothing.

But far above the houses, behind the cloud, something flashed blue-white and was gone.

Madelyn whispered, "Pull the curtains."

He did.

They went upstairs shortly afterward, though neither of them was tired. Madelyn lay rigid beside him. Stan stared at the dark ceiling and calculated contingencies.

If Daphne's child was alive, where would he go? Daphne had no parents living. Hugh Campbell Sr.'s relatives were scattered, ordinary, and, as far as Stan knew, uninformed. Madelyn was the closest blood relation with a stable address.

Stable address. Legal household. No enhanced signature. No public connection beyond sealed records.

A useful place to hide a child.

The thought came to him whole and unwelcome.

No, he decided. Absolutely not.

He turned onto his side.

Madelyn's voice came through the dark. "If they ask, we say no."

"Of course."

"If they insist?"

"They have no authority."

Even as he said it, he knew it was not true. The hidden defense world had many kinds of authority. Some were written down. Some arrived in unmarked aircraft. Some stood in your living room and explained that the public story would be whatever they required it to be.

Madelyn said nothing more.

Near midnight, Stan finally drifted into a shallow, unpleasant sleep.

Outside, Privet Drive did not sleep.

The houses sat in neat rows under the streetlamps. Cars ticked softly as their engines cooled. Curtains remained drawn. No one looked from any window.

On the roof of number four, Queen Maeve crouched beside the chimney and watched the road.

She had been there most of the day, except for the moments when Stan Edgar had nearly noticed her and she had moved faster than his eyes could follow. She wore a dark field coat over armor dulled to prevent reflection. Her hair was tied back. Her face was calm in the way stone is calm after centuries of weather.

She had seen enough.

She had watched Stan leave in the morning with his hard little mouth and his expensive briefcase. She had watched Madelyn through the kitchen window when the woman thought no one could see her; watched her command, flatter, and ignore her own child in rotating measure. She had watched A-Train break a toy truck against the stairs and laugh when his mother called it spirited.

Maeve had been in war zones that felt warmer than number four.

At seven minutes past midnight, the lights along Privet Drive dimmed.

Not out. Not all at once. They dimmed in sequence, as if an invisible hand had turned the street down to a lower setting. The shadows deepened. Security cameras along the road blinked red, then black, then resumed recording nothing at all.

Maeve stood.

At the far corner of the street, Grace Mallory appeared.

She did not arrive with a flash. She did not descend. One moment the pavement was empty; the next she was walking beneath the trees, a long dark coat moving around her knees. She was older than many field operatives and more dangerous than most of them. Her hair was silver-gray, her posture straight, and her eyes missed very little. In one gloved hand she carried a narrow device no larger than a cigarette case. Its surface pulsed once, and another camera died.

Maeve dropped soundlessly from the roof and landed beside the garden wall.

Mallory did not look surprised. "You've been watching."

"All day."

"And?"

Maeve glanced at the house. "You won't like my answer."

"I rarely do."

"They're cold. The man is worse than cold. He's controlled. The woman knows enough to be afraid and not enough to be kind. Their son is already being taught the world exists to applaud him."

Mallory's expression did not change. "That is an assessment, not a recommendation."

"My recommendation is that we find someone else."

"There is no someone else."

"There is always someone else."

"Not for this."

Maeve stepped closer, lowering her voice though the dampened street would have swallowed a shout. "Grace, I watched them. You cannot leave a baby there."

"I can, and I will."

"Because of blood?"

"Because of cover. Because of wards of a sort your armor doesn't measure. Because every hostile sensor still searching for the child will look first in safehouses, academies, hero compounds, GDA medical wings, and off-world evacuation points. They will not look in a cupboard-dry suburban house owned by people who hate everything he is."

Maeve's jaw tightened. "That is not a childhood. That is storage."

Mallory's eyes flicked to her. "He has enemies who would peel this island open to find him."

"Then protect him."

"This is protection."

"No," Maeve said. "This is hiding him and hoping the damage is less visible than the body count."

For the first time, a trace of fatigue touched Mallory's face.

"You think I don't know what I'm doing?"

"I think you know exactly what you're doing. That's what worries me."

The two women stood beneath the quiet lamps, the house before them neat and dark.

At last Mallory looked away. "Homelander is gone."

Maeve inhaled slowly. The name seemed to alter the air around them.

"Confirmed?" she asked.

"As confirmed as anything can be when the body is missing and half the sensors in the county burned out."

"What happened?"

"You've heard the field chatter."

"I've heard twelve versions."

"Then hear thirteen." Mallory's voice remained controlled. "He breached the Campbell safehouse shortly after 2200. Daphne and Hugh were dead before extraction could reach them. The child was in the nursery. Homelander struck him at close range with enough energy to turn the room to glass."

Maeve closed her eyes briefly.

"The strike failed," Mallory continued. "Not deflected by any device we can find. Not absorbed by shielding. Failed. Rebounded, inverted, reacted—we don't have language for it yet. There was a pulse. Local systems died. Viltrumite traces everywhere. When the response team entered, Homelander was gone and the baby was crying under half the ceiling."

Maeve looked toward the bundled darkness of the upper windows. "And Daphne?"

Mallory did not soften. "Daphne made it to the nursery door. Hugh Campbell Sr. was in the hall. They bought him seconds."

Maeve turned away, one hand pressed to the bridge of her nose.

The hidden world had seen losses before. It had buried teams, cities, allies, and secrets. But there was something uniquely cruel about a nursery after an attack. Small socks under ash. A mobile turning above a broken crib. A child alive because everyone else had stood between him and death.

"What about the scar?" Maeve asked.

"Lightning-shaped burn across the forehead. Stable. No spreading. Medics wanted to remove it."

"You said no."

"I said we don't understand it enough to touch it."

A sound rose faintly in the distance.

Maeve looked up.

Mallory slipped the device into her coat. "That's Butcher."

The sound grew into a low mechanical growl, deeper than an engine and rougher than any authorized silent craft. It rolled over the rooftops, dipped, vanished, then returned as a vibrating roar that made the windows of number four tremble in their frames.

A shape dropped out of the cloud.

For one impossible second it seemed to be an ordinary motorcycle, if ordinary motorcycles were the size of small horses and could descend from the sky wrapped in blue anti-gravity wash. Its frame was black and scarred, patched with alien alloys that did not match the original metal. Two stabilizer fins glowed beneath the rear wheel. The headlamp cut through the darkness, then shuttered itself as the bike settled onto the road.

The rider was enormous.

Billy Butcher swung one boot down and killed the engine with a thump of his fist. He had broad shoulders, a dark coat, a beard that looked as though it had survived several fights of its own, and the general air of a man who considered locks, laws, and polite objections to be different names for the same inconvenience. In the crook of one arm he held a bundle wrapped in a gray emergency blanket.

"About bloody time," Mallory said.

"Nice to see you too," Butcher muttered.

Maeve stepped forward. "Is he—?"

"Sleeping." Butcher's voice changed on the word. It became quieter, more careful. "Finally."

Mallory approached. "Any pursuit?"

"Not unless you count three news choppers, two GDA drones that need better manners, and some prick from orbital asking me to identify myself every fifteen seconds."

"You were told to use a silent approach."

"I was told to get the boy here without dropping him. Achieved that, didn't I?"

Mallory gave him a look.

Butcher shifted the bundle protectively. "Safehouse is gone. You know that?"

"I know."

"No, you don't." His eyes were red-rimmed, whether from wind or grief or rage was hard to tell. "You saw a report. I saw the walls. I saw what he did."

Maeve said softly, "Billy."

Butcher looked at her, and for a moment the anger broke into something raw.

"She was by the door," he said. "Daphne. Like she thought she could still get between him and the crib."

Mallory's voice hardened, not unkindly. "This is not the place."

"Where is the place, Grace?" Butcher snapped. "Point me at the room where we're allowed to be angry and I'll happily redecorate it."

Lights flickered in the upstairs window of number six.

Mallory lifted one hand. The light went out again.

Butcher breathed through his nose, looked down at the bundle, and seemed to force himself back from the edge. "He's got her eyes, maybe. Hard to tell. Babies all look like angry old men to me."

Maeve reached toward the blanket. "May I?"

Butcher hesitated, then lowered the child enough for her to see.

Hughie Campbell slept with one fist tucked beside his cheek. His hair was soft and fair-brown under the edge of the blanket. Across his forehead, just above his brow, ran a thin red mark shaped like a crooked bolt of lightning. It did not look like an ordinary cut. The edges shimmered faintly when Maeve leaned close, as if the skin remembered light.

Maeve's face tightened.

"So small," she said.

"Not to hear the whole bloody world talk," Butcher said. "They're already drinking to him in bunkers. Did you know that? Toasting a baby. The child who survived. The little miracle. The end of Homelander. Bunch of sentimental idiots."

"You don't think he's a miracle?" Mallory asked.

Butcher looked at her. "I think he's a baby whose mum and dad are dead."

For once, Mallory had no immediate reply.

From inside number four came the muffled creak of a floorboard. Madelyn or Stan turning in sleeplessness. Maeve glanced toward the house.

"We still have a choice," she said.

Mallory took a sealed envelope from inside her coat. Thick paper. No markings on the outside except the name written in black ink: STAN EDGAR AND MADELYN STILLWELL.

"No," Mallory said. "We have a responsibility."

"Those are not always the same thing."

"They are tonight."

Butcher stared at the envelope. "You sure about this?"

Mallory looked at him sharply. "You questioning orders now?"

"Constantly. Just usually quieter."

"This is the only placement that removes him from the myth."

Butcher gave a humorless laugh. "You think sticking him with those two removes him from anything? That man smells like a locked boardroom. The woman looked through me once at a fundraiser like I was furniture that had learned to swear."

"Good," Mallory said. "They will not parade him."

"They might not love him either."

Mallory's face became still.

"No," she said. "They might not."

Maeve stared at her. "And you can live with that?"

Mallory's gaze went to the sleeping child. In the dim streetlight she looked older, not weaker, but worn in places her reputation usually covered.

"I can live with him alive."

The sentence ended the argument, not because it satisfied anyone, but because there was nothing inside it soft enough to grip.

Butcher looked down at Hughie again. "Daphne would've hated this."

"Daphne asked me once," Mallory said quietly, "if anything happened, to keep him away from the machine as long as possible."

"The machine?"

"Fame. Recruitment. Revenge. Men like Homelander. Men like us." She folded the envelope once against her palm. "She knew what the world would try to make of him."

Maeve said, "A symbol."

"Yes."

Butcher's mouth twisted. "He's already that."

"Then we give him years before he knows it."

The baby stirred. His small face wrinkled. Butcher froze, suddenly terrified in a way no battlefield had made him. Hughie made a soft sound, not quite a cry, and settled again.

"Right," Butcher whispered. "Right, little mate. Easy."

Mallory stepped over the low garden wall and walked up the path to the front door of number four. Maeve followed a pace behind. Butcher came last, carrying Hughie as if the blanket contained something more fragile than glass and more dangerous than a bomb.

At the doorstep, Mallory stopped.

For a moment, none of them moved.

Number four was silent. Behind its walls slept the people who would wake to find their lives altered by a child they had tried not to remember. Above them, clouds covered the stars. Beneath the ordinary pavement, no one would have guessed that hidden satellites were being redirected, agency files sealed, and operatives across the world told to forget every route they had used that night.

Mallory held out the envelope. "Put him down."

Butcher did not.

"Billy."

"Give me a second."

He crouched slowly. His coat brushed the clean doorstep. He adjusted the blanket around Hughie's shoulders, tucking the edges in with surprising gentleness.

"You're gonna hear a lot of rubbish about what you are," he murmured, barely audible. "Don't believe most of it."

Maeve looked away.

Butcher set Hughie on the doorstep.

The child shifted, one tiny hand escaping the blanket. His fingers opened and closed against the cold air. Mallory slipped the envelope beneath the fold where it would be seen the moment anyone lifted him.

"Will they read it?" Maeve asked.

"Stan Edgar will read anything that looks like leverage," Mallory said.

"And if he refuses?"

"He won't."

Butcher straightened. "That's it then?"

"No," Mallory said.

She took a small disc from her pocket and pressed it to the underside of the doorframe. It vanished into the paint as if absorbed.

"Passive monitor?" Maeve asked.

"Emergency beacon. Dormant unless the house is breached by specified signatures."

"Specified?"

"Viltrumite. Homelander-associated energy. Certain Coalition markers. A few others."

Butcher grunted. "And if the threat is neglect?"

Mallory did not look at him. "Then no beacon in the world will help."

He stepped toward her. "Grace—"

"No." Her voice cut the air. "You think I don't see it? You think because I make the call, I don't know the cost? This child is alive in a war that has eaten adults with armies behind them. If his price for surviving the next decade is an unhappy house, then that is a cruel bargain and still better than a grave."

Butcher's fists clenched.

Maeve said, "Enough. You'll wake him."

They all looked down.

Hughie slept.

The anger drained out of Butcher's posture, leaving grief behind. He knelt once more, bent his head, and kissed the baby's forehead beside the lightning scar. His beard brushed Hughie's skin. Hughie made a small, indignant sound.

"Sorry," Butcher whispered. "Scratchy, I know."

He stood quickly and wiped at his eyes with the back of his hand before either woman could comment.

Mallory gave him the mercy of pretending not to notice. "You need to return the bike."

"Borrowed, didn't steal."

"From whom?"

Butcher sniffed. "Someone who owed me."

"That narrows it not at all."

He gave a faint, broken smile. Then it vanished.

Around them, the night held its breath.

Mallory stepped back from the doorstep. Maeve remained a moment longer, looking down at the baby.

"He'll be famous," Maeve said. "No matter what we do."

"One day," Mallory replied.

"There will be memorials. Stories. Children in academy corridors whispering his name. Enemies telling themselves he is the reason their champion fell."

"One day," Mallory repeated.

Maeve's voice lowered. "And when that day comes, what if he asks why we left him here?"

Mallory looked at number four.

"Then I hope he is old enough to hate me and alive enough to do something with it."

Butcher muttered, "Bloody cheerful as ever."

Mallory turned away. "We go."

They walked back down the path. At the wall, Butcher paused and looked over his shoulder. The bundle on the doorstep was barely visible in the shadow of the porch.

"He needs a name in the files," Maeve said.

"He has one," Mallory answered. "Hugh Campbell. Hughie."

"The survivor tags are already spreading."

"I know."

"What will they call him?"

Mallory's expression was unreadable. "Whatever helps them sleep."

Butcher swung onto the motorcycle. The anti-gravity fins lit with a low blue glow. "And what do we call him?"

Mallory glanced once more toward the doorstep.

"A child," she said. "For as long as we can."

Butcher kicked the engine alive. This time the roar was muffled by Mallory's dampening field, reduced to a heavy vibration in the bones. Maeve stepped into the street, preparing to leap to the rooftops.

Before they separated, a distant sound drifted over the houses.

It was not loud. It was not from Privet Drive. Somewhere far beyond the suburb, in hidden rooms, sealed stations, off-world embassies, and safehouses with blacked-out windows, people were celebrating. Not openly. Not on the news. Not in any way that would survive daylight. But the hidden world had learned that Homelander was gone, and the impossible child was alive, and even disciplined people sometimes needed to believe in omens.

Mallory heard it too.

Her mouth tightened. "Careless."

"Human," Maeve said.

"Often the same thing."

Butcher lifted the bike a few inches off the ground. "To Daphne and Hugh," he said roughly.

Maeve bowed her head.

Mallory did not, but her eyes moved to the child on the doorstep.

"To the boy," she said.

Butcher's motorcycle rose into the air and vanished into the low cloud. Maeve sprang upward, caught the edge of a roof, and disappeared across the dark line of houses. Mallory remained alone in the street for several seconds, watching number four.

Then she took out her device and clicked it once.

The streetlamps brightened to their ordinary glow. The cameras resumed recording empty pavement. The night released its held breath.

Grace Mallory turned and walked away.

A soft wind moved through the hedges of Privet Drive. It stirred the blanket around the baby on the doorstep and lifted one corner of the envelope addressed to the people inside.

Hughie Campbell slept on.

He did not know that his parents were dead. He did not know that a monster had tried to kill him and failed. He did not know that, at that very moment, hardened operatives were raising glasses in hidden bases, alien envoys were sending encrypted prayers through orbital relays, and young heroes who had only heard rumors were whispering about a lightning-shaped scar.

He did not know that the house behind him would be cold.

He did not know he was famous.

He did not know that one day letters would find him no matter where he was hidden, or that a rough man on a flying motorcycle would come back for him with a cake and the truth.

For now, he was only a sleeping baby on a spotless suburban doorstep, one small hand resting against the letter beside him, while the secret world quietly marked the beginning of a legend it did not yet understand.

And inside number four, Stan Edgar woke suddenly in the dark, certain he had heard something at the door.
