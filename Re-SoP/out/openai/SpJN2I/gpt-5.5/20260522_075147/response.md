CHAPTER TWO
THE VANISHING GLASS
Nearly ten years had slipped by since the infant had been left on the doorstep of number four, Privet Drive, and the house had done everything in its power to look unchanged.
The same clipped hedges stood at attention, the same polished brass number shone beside the door, and the same pale curtains twitched whenever anything unfamiliar passed along the street.
Inside, the sitting room advertised the life Stan Edgar and Madelyn Stillwell wished everyone to believe they lived: disciplined, successful, respectable, and utterly untouched by strangeness.
There were framed photographs of their son at every stage of his loud and expensive childhood, from pudgy baby in designer trainers to grinning schoolboy in sponsored athletic gear.
In one picture, A-Train sat on a miniature racing scooter; in another, he wore a glossy helmet and posed beside a youth-speed program banner arranged by one of Stan’s contacts.
There were no photographs of Hughie Campbell.
No school picture, no birthday snapshot, no cheap frame on a side table acknowledged that another child slept beneath the same roof.
Hughie was there all the same, folded under a thin blanket in the cupboard beneath the stairs, dreaming of a road that vanished into cloud and of engine noise somewhere above him.
The dream had a machine in it, not quite a motorcycle and not quite an aircraft, with a shape like a black insect and lights that hummed blue under its frame.
He was reaching toward it when the cupboard door rattled so sharply that he struck his elbow on the pipe beside his mattress.
“Up,” Madelyn Stillwell called through the wood, her voice bright with irritation. “Now, Hughie. Don’t make me repeat myself.”
Hughie blinked in the dark, trying to hold on to the sound of the flying engine, but it scattered as quickly as dreams always did.
“I’m up,” he said, though he was still on his side and one sock had disappeared during the night.
“Kitchen,” Madelyn snapped. “Breakfast needs watching. It is A-Train’s birthday, and I will not have you ruining the schedule.”
The word birthday reached him like a cold spoon down the back of his shirt.
Of course it was A-Train’s birthday; the entire week had been filled with deliveries, phone calls, branded wrapping paper, and Stan’s clipped orders about presentation.
Hughie crawled out of the cupboard and dressed in the half-light, pulling on a shirt that had once belonged to A-Train and hung from him like a flag from a pole.
His knees were sharp, his wrists too thin, and his hair stuck up in impossible little tufts no matter how much water Madelyn forced through it.
He found his missing sock under the lowest stair, shook off a dead spider, and put it on without complaint.
The cupboard smelled of dust, old varnish, and the cleaning chemicals Madelyn used everywhere except inside the place where he slept.
When Hughie entered the kitchen, the table had almost disappeared beneath A-Train’s presents.
Boxes of running shoes, gaming hardware, speed-monitor bands, a new tablet, a youth performance watch, training cones, and a miniature aerodynamic bike had been arranged in tiers like a shrine.
Stan Edgar stood by the counter with a business paper folded in one hand and a phone in the other, already dressed for a day he insisted was family time.
He glanced over the top of his reading glasses and said, “Hair.”
Hughie lifted a hand to flatten it.
“Not like that,” Stan said. “Comb it. Properly. You look like you were dragged through a turbine.”
Madelyn gave Hughie a frying pan and pointed at the stove.
“Bacon first. Then eggs. If anything burns, you can explain to A-Train why his birthday began with incompetence.”
Hughie set to work, keeping his fingers clear of spitting grease.
He had learned to move carefully in the kitchen because a burn meant two things: pain, and Madelyn staring at the mark later with narrowed eyes if it faded too quickly.
That had happened before.
Small cuts closed by morning; bruises vanished while A-Train’s were still purple; once, after a scrape from the garden fence, Hughie had watched new skin knit itself smooth while he sat terrified on the bathroom floor.
When he had tried to tell Madelyn, she had scrubbed the place with antiseptic until it hurt again and told him never to invent disgusting stories for attention.
A-Train thundered downstairs just as Hughie slid the eggs onto plates.
He was broad, shiny-faced, and dressed in a new tracksuit with lightning stripes down the sleeves, though Madelyn insisted the design was merely fashionable.
“My presents,” A-Train said, as if greeting beloved relatives.
“Happy birthday, darling,” Madelyn cooed, bending to kiss his cheek.
Stan clapped a hand on his son’s shoulder. “Big year. Testing year. We’ll see what your metrics look like after summer conditioning.”
A-Train barely listened; he was already counting packages.
Hughie placed plates where Madelyn pointed, trying not to knock a tower of boxes with his elbow.
A-Train’s face began to darken.
“Thirty-eight,” he said.
Madelyn froze. “Thirty-eight?”
“Last year was forty.”
“There are additional experiences, sweetheart,” Stan said smoothly. “The facility trial, the private session, the indoor track booking.”
“Those aren’t wrapped.”
Madelyn’s smile sharpened with panic. “We can buy more at the zoo shop. Two, three, however many you want.”
A-Train considered this with the solemnity of a judge being asked to spare a prisoner.
“Four,” he said.
“Four,” Madelyn agreed at once.
Hughie looked down at his breakfast and ate quickly, before anyone remembered he had food.
The phone rang before A-Train had finished tearing through the first layer of wrapping.
Madelyn answered in the hall, her voice sugary for three seconds and then brittle for the rest of the call.
When she came back, her mouth had tightened into the expression Hughie associated with envelopes from school.
“Problem,” she said. “Mrs. McCready has shingles. She can’t take him.”
Stan lowered his paper. “Take whom?”
Madelyn jerked her chin toward Hughie without looking at him. “Him.”
For one wild second Hughie’s chest lifted.
Mrs. McCready lived three streets away in a house that smelled of boiled cabbage, damp wool, and the fifteen little dogs whose portraits covered every wall.
Every birthday, while A-Train was taken somewhere loud and expensive, Hughie was left with her and made to sit on a plastic-covered sofa while she explained each dog’s medical history.
“Leave him here,” A-Train said around a mouthful of toast.
“Absolutely not,” Madelyn replied. “The last time he was unsupervised, the upstairs thermostat reset itself in Celsius and your father’s secure printer jammed for no reason.”
“I didn’t touch it,” Hughie said.
Stan’s eyes moved to him, flat and warning.
Hughie shut his mouth.
“We could call your mother,” Stan said.
“She is not having him,” Madelyn answered. “She still talks about the lamp.”
The lamp had exploded during a dinner visit two years ago after A-Train had locked Hughie in the downstairs bathroom and turned off the light.
Hughie remembered the panic, the sudden pressure in his ears, and then being in the hallway with glass dust glittering around him and no idea how the door had opened.
Everyone else remembered only the ruined lamp.
“I can stay in the cupboard,” Hughie said, because a day alone in the quiet house sounded like paradise.
Madelyn looked as though he had suggested storing meat in the piano.
“And come home to find what? Windows cracked? Wiring melted? Some story about how you didn’t mean it?”
“I don’t melt wiring.”
“Enough,” Stan said.
A-Train had begun making a wounded noise low in his throat, not crying exactly, but preparing to.
“I don’t want him coming,” he said. “He makes things weird.”
Madelyn rushed to him. “No, no, darling, nothing is going to spoil your day.”
Hughie stared at the pan soaking in the sink and tried to make his face blank.
That was the most important skill in number four: blankness.
If he looked angry, Stan called it insolence; if he looked frightened, A-Train enjoyed himself; if he looked hopeful, Madelyn found a way to correct the error.
The doorbell rang, and A-Train’s mood changed at once.
His friend Reggie Polks came in with a wrapped package under one arm and the narrow, eager face of someone who liked being near stronger bullies.
Reggie was the sort of boy who held doors shut from the outside and laughed when someone inside stopped asking to be let out.
The adults spoke in low, irritated bursts while the boys waited.
In the end there was no better solution, or at least no solution Stan considered worth additional effort, so Hughie was told he would accompany them and touch nothing.
Before they left, Stan took him aside by the coat cupboard.
The man crouched just enough that his voice would not carry, though his eyes remained level and cold.
“Listen carefully. Any incident today, any scene, any stunt, any hint of one of your episodes, and you will not see daylight outside school for a month.”
“I don’t do them on purpose,” Hughie whispered.
Stan’s grip closed on his shoulder.
“Then learn not to do them at all.”
The car smelled of new leather and A-Train’s synthetic strawberry energy drink.
Hughie sat in the back between Reggie and the door, knees pressed together, while A-Train sprawled across more than his share of the seat and complained about the zoo being for babies.
“It is a research zoo,” Stan said from the front, as if that improved things. “They have secured exhibits, classified-adjacent partnerships, very good donors.”
Madelyn shot him a warning look.
Stan adjusted his words. “Educational partnerships.”
Hughie turned his face toward the window.
He liked watching reflections slide over the glass; sometimes, when he was tired, he thought he could see other lights behind the ordinary ones, thin blue lines tracing the roads or sparks clinging to aerials on rooftops.
He had never told anyone that.
He had learned long ago that the world contained things adults claimed were impossible right up until they punished him for being near them.
Once, when A-Train and two older boys had chased him across the schoolyard, Hughie had run for the bins and somehow ended on the flat roof above the cafeteria.
He remembered his foot slipping, his stomach dropping, and then a feeling like the air had folded under him.
The head teacher accused him of climbing drainpipes; Stan accused him of attention-seeking; Madelyn asked in a very quiet voice whether anyone had seen him jump.
Another time, A-Train had slammed his hand in the back door and Hughie had watched the swelling go down before dinner.
Madelyn wrapped the fingers anyway and told him to keep them hidden.
There had been smaller things, too: a locked drawer open when he desperately needed his confiscated homework, a falling glass stopping just long enough for him to catch it, a street dog staring at him as though it understood every word he did not say.
Hughie did not know what these events meant.
He only knew they frightened the Edgar-Stillwell household more than they frightened him.
The research zoo stood beyond a ring of security fencing disguised with cheerful murals of animals in lab coats.
Its sign showed a smiling giraffe, but the cameras above the ticket booths followed people with a precision that made Hughie’s scalp prickle.
Stan bought premium entry passes for everyone except Hughie, whose wristband was plain gray instead of blue.
A-Train and Reggie received enormous frozen drinks before they were five steps inside.
When the vendor asked Hughie what flavor he wanted, Madelyn began to say he did not need one, but the woman was already waiting with her scoop poised.
Stan, aware of the family behind them, bought Hughie the smallest lemon ice in the freezer.
It tasted of chemicals and sugar and cold sunlight.
For the first hour, Hughie almost enjoyed himself.
They passed a habitat where silver-feathered monkeys dismantled puzzle boxes faster than the keepers could replace them, and a pond where translucent fish flashed like living data screens.
A placard near the aviary explained that several species had been recovered after “upper-atmospheric migratory anomalies,” which Stan read and immediately steered everyone past.
A-Train was bored by anything he could not race, ride, or order to perform.
He shouted at a sleeping armored rhino until a keeper asked him to step back from the rail.
Stan made a donation on the spot and the keeper apologized to A-Train.
At lunch, A-Train complained that his dessert sphere had too little vapor coming off it, so Madelyn bought another and Hughie was allowed the collapsed remains of the first.
It fizzed unpleasantly against his tongue.
By early afternoon, the sky had turned bright and hard, and even Reggie had grown tired of pretending the day was thrilling.
Then A-Train saw the sign for the reptile and xenofauna house.
“Venom things,” he said. “Finally.”
The building was low, dark, and cooler than the paths outside.
Inside, glass-fronted enclosures lined the walls, glowing with careful heat lamps and strips of blue-white containment light.
Ordinary snakes coiled beside creatures that were not quite snakes at all: ribbon-bodied things with too many eyes, shelled lizards breathing mist, a motionless black coil labeled OFF-WORLD SPECIMEN: DO NOT TAP.
A-Train tapped until Madelyn told him not to do it where staff could see.
Hughie drifted behind the others, reading plaques.
Some used words like “acquired,” “recovered,” and “quarantine lineage,” which sounded to him like ways of avoiding the word captured.
At the end of the room stood a tall enclosure with curved glass thicker than Hughie’s hand.
A great serpent lay inside, looped over a heated stone beneath artificial branches.
It was longer than Stan’s car and patterned in dark bronze scales that shimmered green whenever it breathed.
The sign called it a boa-form constrictor, terrestrial-compatible, probable non-native cognition markers, and beneath that, in friendlier letters, CHILDREN: PLEASE DO NOT STRIKE THE GLASS.
“Make it move,” A-Train demanded.
Stan checked whether any staff were watching and rapped two knuckles against the enclosure.
The serpent did not stir.
“Harder,” said Reggie.
Stan gave one sharper tap, then stepped back, irritated that the animal had failed to justify the outing.
A-Train pressed both hands to the glass and pulled a face. “Useless.”
They moved toward a case of venom-spitting lizards.
Hughie remained where he was.
He felt sorry for the serpent in a way that embarrassed him.
Its enclosure was clean and expensive, but it was still a box, and all day people came to knock, stare, demand, and leave.
“I know,” Hughie murmured, barely moving his lips. “People are awful.”
The serpent opened one eye.
Hughie went still.
The eye was gold around a vertical black slit, and it regarded him not like an animal noticing movement but like a person interrupted during a private thought.
A second eyelid slid sideways and vanished.
Then, very slowly, the serpent lifted its head until it was level with Hughie’s face.
Hello, small warm thing, said a voice.
Hughie’s heart kicked against his ribs.
The sound had not come through the air, exactly; it had arrived behind his ears, shaped from hiss and pressure and meaning.
He looked left and right.
No one else had reacted.
“Did you—” Hughie began, then swallowed. “Did you say hello?”
The serpent’s tongue flickered, tasting the glass between them.
You heard. That is unusual.
Hughie stepped closer, drawn by wonder before fear could stop him.
“I didn’t know I could.”
Most cannot. Most only pound on the invisible wall and bare their little teeth.
Hughie glanced toward A-Train.
“He does that.”
The serpent’s head tilted.
The loud one belongs to you?
“No,” Hughie said quickly, and then, because the answer seemed complicated, added, “I live in his house.”
A shiver passed through the great coils.
That is not the same as belonging.
Hughie felt something tighten in his throat.
The serpent’s gaze dropped to his forehead, where his hair never quite hid the pale lightning mark.
Old burn, it said.
Hughie touched the scar before he could stop himself.
“I’ve always had it.”
Old sky-fire.
“What does that mean?”
The serpent did not answer at once.
Behind Hughie, Reggie shouted, “Oi, A-Train, he’s talking to that thing.”
Hughie jumped back.
A-Train came pounding over, eager for any excuse to shove him.
“What are you doing?” he demanded.
“Nothing.”
“You were whispering.”
“No, I wasn’t.”
A-Train shouldered him aside hard enough that Hughie struck the low rail in front of the enclosure.
Pain flashed along his hip.
The serpent’s head drew back.
Do not, it said, and this time the words struck like a warning bell.
A-Train flattened his nose to the glass. “Move, freak.”
Reggie laughed.
Hughie’s face burned.
He was not sure whether the freak was meant for him or the serpent; in number four, it usually meant him.
A-Train lifted a fist as if to bang the barrier again.
Hughie wanted, with a sudden force that scared him, for there to be no barrier between the serpent and the people tormenting it.
The containment lights flickered.
The glass made a sound like ice dropped into boiling water.
For a heartbeat, fine white lines raced across the surface, not cracks exactly but seams of pale static, mapping the whole curved pane.
Then the glass was gone.
It did not fall, break, or explode.
It simply ceased to be there, leaving A-Train and Reggie leaning into empty air.
Both boys screamed and threw themselves backward.
The serpent uncoiled with astonishing speed.
Its body poured over the edge of the enclosure in a shining bronze river, scales whispering against metal and tile.
Someone near the lizard cases shrieked.
A keeper shouted into a radio.
Madelyn seized A-Train by the back of his tracksuit and dragged him behind her, while Stan moved first toward his son and then, seeing the serpent’s size, behind a display about venom safety.
Hughie sat on the floor where he had fallen, unable to move.
The great head passed inches from him.
For one impossible second, the serpent paused.
Thank you, scar-child, it said.
“I didn’t mean to,” Hughie whispered.
Few cages open because someone means it, replied the serpent.
Then it slid toward the emergency exit, which burst outward before it touched the bar.
Sunlight flooded the reptile house.
Alarms began to wail, not the cheerful fire-alarm sound Hughie knew from school drills but a rising electronic howl that made the containment lights flash red.
Visitors ran in every direction.
A lizard spat harmless blue foam at the ceiling.
A recorded voice ordered everyone to remain calm while every adult in the room proved the instruction impossible.
By the time security staff in gray armor arrived, the serpent had vanished into the service gardens beyond the building.
A woman with a tranquilizer rifle kept asking who had authorized the release.
The reptile supervisor stared at the empty enclosure and repeated, “There is no breach pattern. There is no breach pattern.”
Stan spoke to the zoo director in a voice Hughie had heard him use on corporate calls after something dangerous had happened and needed to become someone else’s fault.
Madelyn held A-Train’s face between both hands and told him he was brave, brave, so brave.
A-Train, who had not been touched, announced that the monster had tried to eat him.
Reggie improved the story by claiming it had looked directly at his throat.
Hughie said nothing.
He expected someone to mention that A-Train had been hitting the glass.
Instead, Reggie’s eyes slid toward him.
“He was talking to it,” Reggie said. “Before it got out. I heard him.”
The adults turned.
Hughie felt the air leave the room, though the emergency doors still stood open.
“I wasn’t,” he said, too quietly.
A-Train pointed at him with trembling delight. “He was. He made it happen.”
Madelyn’s expression changed from fear to something colder.
Stan did not shout at the zoo.
He did not shout in the car, either, not with Reggie still in the back seat and A-Train performing near-death for an audience.
He drove with both hands locked on the wheel while Madelyn made short, sharp phone calls about trauma, refunds, and confidentiality.
Hughie sat against the door and watched the city smear past.
His hip had stopped hurting already.
That frightened him almost as much as the vanished glass.
When they dropped Reggie at his house, he leaned down to the car window and grinned at Hughie.
“Snake boy,” he mouthed.
The instant the front door of number four closed, Stan’s control snapped into silence rather than noise.
He removed his coat, hung it precisely on the hook, and turned.
“Cupboard,” he said.
“I didn’t do anything.”
“Cupboard.”
“The glass just—”
Stan crossed the hall in two strides.
Hughie flinched before the hand reached him, which made A-Train snicker from behind Madelyn.
“You will not use that tone,” Stan said softly. “You will not tell stories. You will not embarrass this family in public with another abnormal episode.”
“I don’t know how it happened.”
“That,” Madelyn said, her voice shaking, “is the problem.”
The cupboard door opened.
Hughie stepped inside because not stepping inside only ever made things worse.
Stan pointed at the mattress.
“No dinner. No lights. No noises. If I hear one word from you, tomorrow begins the same way.”
The door shut, and the bolt slid home.
For a long time Hughie sat in the dark with his knees tucked to his chest.
The house settled around him: pipes ticking, A-Train laughing at something on television, Madelyn speaking too brightly on the phone to some other mother about the awful incident at the zoo.
Hughie pressed his fingers to his forehead.
The scar felt no different: a thin raised line, shaped like a little fork of lightning, almost hidden under his hair.
Old sky-fire, the serpent had called it.
He did not know why those words made the dark seem larger.
His aunt and uncle—he had been told to call them that, though Madelyn said it was only a family arrangement—had always said his parents died in a car accident.
Hughie had no memory of them.
Sometimes, when he was half asleep in the cupboard, he saw flashes that did not fit any road he had ever known: a ceiling breaking open, a white-gold glare, a woman’s voice, a man shouting his name though nobody in the Edgar-Stillwell house ever said it that way.
Then came heat on his forehead and the sensation of falling through light.
He had decided, because children must decide something, that those were pieces of the crash.
But car crashes did not explain flying motorcycles in dreams.
They did not explain strangers who stared at him in shopping centers and then looked away with tears in their eyes.
Once, a woman in a silver coat had touched two fingers to her brow as he passed, like a salute, and disappeared into an alley that ended in a brick wall.
Another time, an old man with a metal hand had bought him a chocolate bar from a station vending machine, called him “little survivor,” and vanished before Stan returned from buying coffee.
Madelyn had thrown the chocolate away.
Hughie had never told anyone about the small drone that hovered outside his school window one rainy afternoon, its lens fixed on his scar until he blinked and it was gone.
He had certainly never told anyone that animals sometimes seemed to listen when he was lonely.
Tonight, though, the secret felt different.
The serpent had not merely listened.
It had spoken, and Hughie had understood.
Somewhere beyond Privet Drive, beyond the locked cupboard, beyond Stan Edgar’s rules and Madelyn Stillwell’s careful lies, a bronze creature was sliding through grass under the open sky because the glass had vanished.
Hughie did not know whether he had saved it or doomed it.
He did not know whether he should feel guilty, proud, afraid, or all three.
His stomach growled, and he wrapped both arms around it.
From the sitting room came A-Train’s voice, already telling the story bigger than before: the serpent had lunged for him, he had dodged with natural reflexes, security had been useless.
Stan made a low approving sound at the word reflexes.
Hughie lay down on the thin mattress and stared at the dark underside of the stairs.
He wondered whether the serpent had a name.
He wondered whether it would find its way somewhere warm.
He wondered, not for the first time, whether anyone had ever looked for him the way he imagined looking for it.
The thought was dangerous, so he pushed it away.
In number four, hope was another kind of glass: invisible most of the time, easy to bruise yourself against, and gone in an instant when something inside you struck too hard.
Still, before sleep took him, Hughie heard again the voice behind his ears.
That is not the same as belonging.
He held the words close in the cupboard darkness, not understanding them yet, but knowing they were truer than anything the Edgar-Stillwell household had ever told him.
