CHAPTER ONE

THE BOY WHO SURVIVED

Stan Edgar and Madelyn Stillwell, of number four, Privet Drive, considered themselves models of sober, respectable modern life. They lived in a clean square house with trimmed hedges, polished windows, discreet security cameras, and nothing in the garden that might provoke comment from neighbors. They believed in practical things: corporate advancement, private schooling, controlled messaging, good tailoring, and never, under any circumstances, being noticed by the wrong people.

The wrong people, in Stan Edgar’s view, included reporters, regulators, unstable shareholders, former employees with grudges, and anyone wearing armor in daylight.

Stan was a senior executive at Vought International’s British operations, where he was paid handsomely to make dangerous things sound reliable. He was precise, severe, narrow-eyed, and always dressed as though he expected to be photographed in a crisis. Madelyn Stillwell was blonde, polished, and careful in the way of someone who could smile warmly while deciding how best to remove a person from a room. She spent much of her time watching the neighbors, not because she liked them, but because she believed in knowing what everyone else might say before they said it.

Their son, A-Train, was still small, but already treated like a brand in development. He had private tutors, performance coaches, special shoes, special drinks, and a bedroom large enough to contain more toys than several ordinary children could have imagined. In Stan and Madelyn’s opinion, there was no finer boy in the world, and if there was, they would have wanted him audited.

The Edgar-Stillwell household had almost everything it wanted. It also had a family secret.

Madelyn had once had a sister named Daphne Campbell. She did not mention Daphne if she could help it, and if anyone else mentioned her, Madelyn’s face acquired the still, blank expression of a closed boardroom door. Daphne had married Hugh Campbell Sr., a man Stan regarded as reckless, sentimental, and fatally connected to circles where people used words like “mission,” “shielding,” “orbital relay,” and “classified evacuation” without irony. The Campbells had a baby son, too. Hughie, he was called. Stan had never seen the child and had no wish to. Children like that came with stories attached, and stories had a way of attracting investigators, memorialists, and worse.

On the gray morning when this story begins, nothing about Privet Drive suggested that Britain’s hidden defense world had spent the previous night staring at its monitors in shock. The sky hung low and flat over the neat roofs. Cars reversed from driveways. Milk bottles clinked. Curtains twitched. Somewhere, a dog barked at a delivery van.

Inside number four, Stan selected a tie the color of wet cement, checked his watch, checked his cufflinks, and listened to Madelyn coaxing A-Train into his chair.

A-Train was not in a coaxable mood. He kicked the table leg, slapped a spoonful of cereal onto the floor, and shouted that he wanted his racing shoes.

“You had them yesterday,” said Madelyn, with the bright patience she reserved for her son and no one else.

“I want them again.”

“You’ll have them after breakfast.”

“I want them now.”

Stan glanced at the mess on the floor and gave a thin smile. “Competitive temperament.”

Madelyn smiled back. “Exactly.”

Neither of them noticed the black courier drone that drifted past the kitchen window, paused for three seconds as though scanning the house, and then zipped away over the roofs.

At half past eight Stan stepped out with his briefcase, gave Madelyn a quick kiss, and avoided A-Train’s sticky hands with the reflexes of a man accustomed to preserving expensive fabric. He crossed the drive, unlocked his car, and was backing neatly into the road when he saw the first thing that spoiled the morning.

At the corner of Privet Drive, beside the street sign, stood a man in a long dark coat with a silver stripe down one sleeve. He was looking up at the sign as though it had just confirmed something important. At his feet rested a hard black case, the kind used by military technicians or field medics. While Stan watched, a second person joined him: a woman in reflective glasses, boots too heavy for suburban pavement, and a collar that seemed to pulse faintly blue.

Stan braked.

The man with the case looked directly toward number four.

For a moment Stan had the unpleasant sensation that the stranger was not looking at the car, or the house, but at something behind the walls. Then another commuter honked, and Stan realized he had stopped halfway into the road.

He drove on.

By the time he looked in the mirror, the two people were gone.

Stan told himself they were utility contractors. Possibly telecommunications. Possibly filming something. There were always people filming something now. He disliked filming. Filming meant permits, crowds, questions, and people with lanyards.

He turned onto the main road and forced his thoughts back to work. There was a strategy meeting at nine thirty, a risk review at eleven, and a set of casualty projections that needed to be made more presentable before anyone from legal saw them. These were proper concerns. Measurable concerns. Concerns with folders.

But the city did not cooperate.

At the first traffic light, a formation of small drones crossed above the intersection. They were not hobby drones. They moved too smoothly, black against the pale morning, each one no bigger than a pigeon and each bearing a tiny white insignia Stan knew well enough to pretend he did not recognize. Two pedestrians stopped and pointed. A bus driver leaned toward his windscreen. Someone laughed uncertainly.

The drones banked east, then vanished so fast they seemed to have been pulled through a hole in the air.

Stan tightened his grip on the steering wheel.

A few streets later, traffic slowed almost to a stop. Ahead, outside a bakery, a cluster of oddly dressed people stood in the open with the absurd confidence of those who thought ordinary rules had been suspended. One wore a navy tactical cloak over a business suit. Another had a metallic brace coiled around one arm. A third, an elderly woman with silver hair, wore what looked unmistakably like segmented body armor beneath a raincoat.

Stan despised people who made spectacles of themselves. He despised theatricality. He especially despised theatricality attached to risk.

As his car crept past, he caught a fragment of excited conversation through the half-open window of the vehicle beside him.

“—Campbells, both of them—”

“—Homelander’s gone, that’s the word—”

“—no, the baby lived—”

Stan’s foot slipped on the brake.

The car behind him honked. Stan drove on, face set and colorless.

There were many Campbells, he told himself. Campbell was not an uncommon name. Homelander was always generating rumors; the man’s shadow fell across too many classified budgets for silence ever to hold completely. And a baby? People always added a baby to a rumor. It made everyone lean closer.

Still, Stan spent the next ten minutes imagining Madelyn’s sister Daphne: her stubborn expression, her refusal to listen, her friends in sealed rooms and impossible vehicles. Daphne, who had married Hugh Campbell Sr. and vanished into a life Madelyn insisted was beneath pity and beyond discussion. Daphne, who had a baby.

Hughie.

No. Stan had work.

At Vought’s London offices, he parked in his reserved space, walked past security without acknowledging the guard, and took the lift to his floor. He always sat with his back to the window. He had chosen the office for that reason. Windows invited distraction, and Stan Edgar had built his career on controlling the direction of his attention.

If he had turned around that morning, he would have seen three pale streaks high above the city, too fast for planes and too deliberate for meteors. He would have seen a police helicopter abruptly veer away from empty air. He would have seen office workers gathering near windows all along the street, pointing at the sky and checking their phones.

Stan saw none of it.

He chaired the strategy meeting without raising his voice. He corrected two projections, dismantled a junior executive’s optimism, and reminded a roomful of adults that hope was not a compliance framework. He was almost calm by lunch.

Then he went out for coffee.

The pavement outside was busier than usual. A pair of men in gray overcoats stood near the entrance to an alleyway, speaking into no visible devices. A courier in a motorcycle helmet with no motorcycle in sight leaned against a lamppost. A woman with dark braided hair and a gold insignia at her throat turned as Stan passed and went quiet.

He walked faster.

At the coffee shop counter, while waiting for his order, he heard someone behind him whisper, “They say the child took the strike.”

Another voice: “A Viltrumite strike? That’s impossible.”

“Tell that to the scar.”

Stan took his coffee too quickly and burned his fingers.

He did not turn around. Stan believed that if one did not acknowledge a thing, one retained the option of denying it later. He left the shop, crossed the street, and had nearly reached the Vought entrance when a short, broad man in a violet raincoat stepped into his path.

Stan almost collided with him.

“Careful,” said Stan coldly.

But the man did not seem offended. His face shone with such wild relief that for one dreadful second Stan thought he might embrace him.

“Careful?” said the man. “My dear civilian, careful’s what we’ve all been for years. Not today. Today the butcher-bird’s fallen out of the sky.”

“I have no idea what you mean,” Stan said.

“Homelander,” whispered the man, with reverence and fear twisted together. “Gone. And the Campbell boy still breathing. If you knew what that meant, you’d be dancing in that expensive coat.”

Stan stared at him.

The man winked, patted Stan’s sleeve, and moved away into the crowd.

Stan did not remember returning to his office. He did remember shutting the door, lowering the blinds, and standing very still beside his desk with the coffee cooling untouched in his hand.

At three o’clock an internal alert came across a restricted Vought channel. Stan saw only the header before it vanished from his screen:

UNCONFIRMED GLOBAL DEFENSE ACTIVITY: VILTRUMITE-CLASS INCIDENT

Then the system refreshed, and the message was gone.

At five sharp, Stan left. He did not say good night to his assistant. He did not check the evening reports. He walked to his car through a city that seemed to be pretending badly. People gathered in doorways. Sirens sounded and stopped without approaching. Above the rooftops, a dim green pulse flickered against the clouds and disappeared.

When he reached Privet Drive, the first thing he saw was another drone.

It hovered above the garden wall of number four, no larger than a blackbird, its lens fixed on the front door.

Stan slammed the car door.

The drone tilted. For one instant its central eye glowed blue. Then it darted upward and vanished over the roof.

Stan stood on the drive, breathing hard through his nose. He considered calling security. He considered calling Vought’s private response line. He considered calling no one, because calls were records and records were liabilities.

In the end, he went inside.

Madelyn had spent what she called a productive day. A-Train had bitten a playgroup assistant, which she described as assertiveness under pressure. A neighbor had asked whether they had heard strange aircraft overnight, which Madelyn described as people needing hobbies. The dry cleaner had misplaced one of Stan’s shirts, which she described as unacceptable.

Stan listened through dinner, nodding at the correct intervals. A-Train banged a toy car against the table leg until Madelyn promised him a better one.

At last, when A-Train had been put to bed after prolonged negotiation, Stan and Madelyn sat in the living room. The television murmured in the corner. Stan had intended not to mention anything. Mentioning gave shape. Shape gave leverage.

Then the evening news changed.

“And finally,” said the presenter, wearing the strained smile of someone asked to sound amused by a thing that had frightened the newsroom, “viewers across Britain have reported unusual lights in the sky overnight and again this afternoon. Authorities say the flashes were likely the result of atmospheric testing, though no agency has yet claimed responsibility. There have also been numerous reports of unidentified drones operating in daylight over London, Birmingham, Manchester, and several coastal areas.”

The screen changed to shaky phone footage. A dark shape crossed a bright patch of cloud and vanished with a snap of light.

Madelyn stopped stirring her tea.

“Social media users have suggested everything from military exercises to superhero activity,” the presenter continued. “Officials urge the public not to speculate.”

Stan reached for the remote and turned the television off.

The silence afterward was worse.

Madelyn looked at him. “What have you heard?”

“Nothing reliable.”

“That isn’t what I asked.”

Stan removed his glasses, cleaned them with a cloth, and put them back on. “There were people in town today. Not civilians.”

Madelyn’s face tightened.

“They were talking,” he said. “About the Campbells.”

Madelyn set down her cup very carefully. “No.”

“And Homelander.”

“No.”

“And a child.”

The room seemed to shrink around them.

Madelyn rose and crossed to the window. The curtains were already closed, but she parted them with two fingers and looked out into the street. “Daphne made her choices.”

“I’m aware.”

“She wanted that life. She wanted those people. She wanted secrets and danger and men with files instead of souls.”

Stan almost smiled at that, because Madelyn had spent most of her professional life among men with files instead of souls. But he said nothing.

“If something has happened,” Madelyn said, “it has nothing to do with us.”

“That may depend on whether anyone else agrees.”

She turned from the window. “We are not taking in her consequences.”

“Lower your voice.”

“I mean it, Stan.”

“So do I.”

For a moment neither of them spoke. Upstairs, A-Train shouted in his sleep and kicked the wall.

Madelyn pressed a hand to her forehead. “Did anyone say Hughie’s name?”

Stan hesitated.

“Stan.”

“Yes.”

Madelyn’s eyes closed.

“It may be rumor,” he said. “Most of what circulates around Homelander is rumor.”

“You don’t believe that.”

Stan did not answer.

Madelyn sat down again, but not as she usually did. She perched on the edge of the chair as if ready to stand, run, deny, or all three. “If they come here—”

“They won’t.”

“If they do—”

“They won’t.”

But Stan did not sleep quickly that night.

He lay awake beside Madelyn, listening to the hum of the heating system, the occasional creak of the house, the distant hiss of tires on wet asphalt. Once, just after eleven, a low vibration passed through the walls. Madelyn stirred but did not wake. Stan got up and went to the window.

Privet Drive lay dark and orderly beneath the streetlamps.

At the far corner stood a woman.

She had not been there a second before. Stan was certain of it. She wore a long dark coat and stood with both hands folded over the head of a cane. Even at a distance he could tell she was old, though not frail. Her white hair was pulled back. Her posture was military-straight. Her face was turned toward number four.

Stan stepped back from the curtain.

When he looked again, the street appeared empty.

He told himself he had seen a neighbor. An old woman walking late. Nothing more.

Outside, at the end of Privet Drive, Grace Mallory watched the bedroom curtain fall back into place.

“Still awake,” said a voice from the shadows.

Mallory did not turn. “Men like Edgar rarely sleep when there is no profitable answer to a problem.”

Queen Maeve stepped from the darkness beside the garden wall. She wore no costume, only a dark coat over plain clothes, but there were people who could have hidden in burlap and still looked like they belonged on a battlefield. Maeve was one of them. Her red hair was tied back. Her eyes were fixed on the house.

“I’ve watched them since morning,” Maeve said. “They’re exactly what the file said. Worse, maybe.”

“Most people are worse at home.”

“The boy kicked his mother because breakfast came in the wrong bowl.”

Mallory’s expression did not change. “That would be A-Train.”

“That’s what they’re calling him? He’s five.”

“They’re ambitious.”

“They’re poisonous.”

Mallory leaned on her cane and looked at number four. The house was silent now. Square, trim, smug. A perfect civilian hiding place, if one judged only by appearances.

Maeve’s voice dropped. “You’re really going to leave him here.”

“For now.”

“With them.”

“With his only blood relatives available to us in this jurisdiction.”

“Daphne hated her sister’s life.”

“Daphne made many things clear in her contingency statements. She did not anticipate every possibility.”

Maeve looked sharply at her. “She anticipated Homelander.”

At that name, the street seemed colder.

Neither woman spoke for several seconds. Somewhere far away, an aircraft changed course with a muted roar. Above the clouds, invisible to ordinary eyes, satellites adjusted their gaze.

Mallory said, “No one anticipated last night.”

Maeve’s hands curled. “He tore through the safehouse shields like paper.”

“Yes.”

“Hugh and Daphne held him off long enough to get the baby into the inner room.”

“Yes.”

“And then?”

Mallory’s face was carved from stone, but her voice carried fatigue older than the night. “Then Homelander struck.”

Maeve swallowed. She had been told the facts. Facts were not the same as images, and the images had followed her all day: broken walls, scorched floor, the smell of ionized metal, Daphne Campbell’s body between the nursery and the door, Hugh Campbell Sr. with one hand still reaching for the emergency beacon, and the baby alive in the center of a crater burned into reinforced concrete.

“The strike should have killed him,” Maeve said.

“It should have vaporized him.”

“But it didn’t.”

“No.”

“And Homelander?”

“Gone.”

“Dead?”

Mallory did not answer at once. “There is no body.”

Maeve gave a bitter little laugh. “There’s never a body when you need one.”

“There was enough residual energy to suggest catastrophic rebound. Every Viltrumite-reactive sensor in the hemisphere screamed at once. His network went dark. His collaborators are scattering. For the moment, that is what we have.”

“For the moment,” Maeve repeated.

Mallory looked up at the sky. The clouds were low, but beyond them the hidden machinery of the world was awake: Coalition monitors, GDA satellites, emergency relays, quiet ships holding high orbit. “The world believes it has had a fortunate accident. Let it.”

“The defense world doesn’t believe in accidents.”

“No,” said Mallory. “Which is why the child must disappear from it.”

Maeve turned back to the house. “He’ll be famous.”

“He already is.”

“Every academy instructor, every field team, every alien envoy with access to last night’s packet is speaking his name.”

“Which is precisely the danger.”

“He’s a baby, Grace.”

Mallory’s hand tightened on her cane. It was the only sign the words had reached her. “Yes.”

“He has no one left.”

“He has us.”

“Then let us take him.”

“And raise him where? In a GDA facility? Under glass? With analysts measuring his pulse every time he cries? With cadets lining up to glimpse the scar? With enemies searching every classified nursery on Earth?”

Maeve had no answer ready.

Mallory continued, quieter. “He survived Homelander. That makes him a symbol before he is old enough to know his own name. Symbols are useful to causes and fatal to children.”

“And Privet Drive is your solution?”

“For eleven years, perhaps ten if circumstances require. Long enough for the noise to settle. Long enough for him to grow without ceremonies and memorial plaques and frightened people calling him savior.”

Maeve’s mouth tightened. “You think Edgar and Stillwell will love him?”

“No.”

“Then why—”

“I think they will hide him,” Mallory said. “They will hate attention more than they dislike the child. They will keep him out of sight because secrecy is the one virtue they understand.”

“That is a cold calculation.”

“Yes.”

Maeve studied her. “Daphne trusted you.”

Mallory closed her eyes briefly. “Daphne is dead.”

The words fell flat onto the pavement.

Before Maeve could respond, a low rumble reached them from the distance. It was too deep for a car and too irregular for a helicopter. It grew, faded, then grew again, rolling over the rooftops like thunder trapped inside metal.

Maeve looked up.

Mallory slipped a small device from her coat and pressed it once. The nearest streetlamps flickered, dimmed, and settled into a dull amber haze. Security cameras along Privet Drive tilted toward blank walls. A dog began barking two streets away, then abruptly stopped.

The rumble became a roar.

Something dropped through the cloud cover.

It was a motorcycle, though no civilian mechanic would have recognized half of what had been bolted, welded, fused, and grown across its frame. Alien anti-gravity vanes shimmered beneath the wheels. Blue-white stabilizers spat sparks along the rear forks. The engine casing bore scorch marks, old bullet dents, and a sticker in faded red letters that read KEEP BACK UNLESS YOU’RE BULLETPROOF.

The machine descended too fast, corrected itself with a violent sideways lurch, and landed in the middle of Privet Drive with a noise that would have awakened the whole street if Mallory’s dampening field had not swallowed it.

Billy Butcher swung one heavy boot onto the pavement.

He was broad, bearded, wind-battered, and dressed in a long dark coat that looked as though it had survived fires, bar fights, and at least one explosion. His eyes were red-rimmed. His expression dared the world to mention it.

In the crook of one arm, wrapped in a gray emergency blanket, slept a baby.

“You’re late,” Mallory said.

“Had a bit of traffic,” Butcher replied. His voice was rougher than usual. “Orbital cordon over Bristol. Two drones tried to tail me past Reading. One of ’em’s in a pond now.”

Maeve stepped forward. “Is he hurt?”

Butcher looked down at the bundle. For the first time, his face changed. The anger remained, but something raw moved beneath it. “No. Not hurt. Not how he should be.”

Mallory held out her arms. Butcher did not hand the child over at once.

“Billy,” she said.

He swallowed and shifted the blanket back.

Hughie Campbell slept with one tiny fist pressed against his cheek. He had soft fair hair, a round face flushed from sleep, and the solemn exhaustion of a child who had cried until there was nothing left. Across his forehead, just above his brow, ran a mark like a bolt of lightning burned in pale gold and red. It was not an ordinary wound. It seemed to catch what little light remained and hold it under the skin.

Maeve inhaled sharply.

“That’s where it hit?” she whispered.

Butcher nodded once. “Aye.”

Mallory looked at the scar for a long time. “Has it changed?”

“Med team said it cooled after the first hour. Stopped throwing off readings by dawn. Still tripped every Viltrumite sensor they put near him.”

“And his vitals?”

“Strong. Stronger than they liked.” Butcher gave a joyless smile. “Little lad made three machines quit before breakfast.”

Maeve reached out, then stopped herself before touching him. “Daphne would have laughed at that.”

Butcher’s jaw worked.

Mallory glanced at him. “Report.”

The word was cruel, perhaps, but necessary. Butcher seemed to understand that. He looked away from the baby and stared down the empty street.

“Safehouse was gone by the time I got there. Upper floors collapsed inward. Shield lattice burned black. Hugh Campbell Sr. was in the corridor. Daphne was by the inner door.” His voice thickened, but he forced it on. “Homelander’s entry path came straight through the western wall. No hesitation. He knew the layout.”

Maeve’s face hardened. “Inside help.”

“Looks that way.”

Mallory’s eyes sharpened. “Names?”

“Not yet.”

“Get them.”

“I plan to.”

“That is not an instruction to start a private war tonight.”

Butcher looked at her then, and for a moment the street seemed too small to contain him. “They butchered my friends.”

“Yes,” Mallory said.

“He went there for the baby.”

“Yes.”

“He killed Daphne and Hugh to get to him.”

“Yes.”

“And now you want to put him on a doorstep.”

Mallory did not flinch. “I want him alive when he is old enough to ask why.”

Butcher looked toward number four. His expression made clear what he thought of the house, the hedges, the curtains, and everyone inside. “They’ll ruin him.”

“They’ll bore him,” Mallory said. “They’ll hide him. They’ll lie. But they are unlikely to sell him to Viltrumite loyalists or display him at a memorial rally.”

“That’s your standard?”

“Tonight, yes.”

Maeve folded her arms. “Grace, listen to yourself.”

“I have been doing nothing else for twelve hours.”

A soft sound came from the bundle. Hughie shifted, opened his mouth, and made a small, indignant noise. Butcher immediately looked down.

“Easy, wee man,” he muttered, voice suddenly low. “Easy. You’re all right.”

The baby’s eyes fluttered but did not open. His fist closed around a fold of blanket.

Mallory watched Butcher’s large hand hover helplessly above him. “You may say goodbye.”

Butcher gave a short laugh. “That supposed to be kindness?”

“It is what I can offer.”

He bent his head. The kiss he placed on the baby’s hair was awkward, careful, and barely there.

“Your mum was brave as any bastard I ever knew,” he whispered. “Your dad too. Don’t let anyone tell you different.”

Maeve turned away for a second, jaw tight.

Mallory took the baby at last. Hughie was lighter than he looked. Warm. Alive. It was a fact so unlikely that even she, who had spent a life accepting unlikely facts, felt the weight of it.

From her coat she withdrew a sealed envelope. It was heavy, cream-colored, and marked only with Stan Edgar and Madelyn Stillwell’s names. Inside were the facts they would need, the warnings they would resent, and enough legal authority to make refusal inconvenient. Not impossible. Nothing was impossible for people determined to be cruel. But inconvenient.

Maeve saw the envelope and shook her head. “A letter.”

“Yes.”

“You think a letter explains Daphne dead, Hugh dead, Homelander gone, and a baby with a strike scar?”

“No. But it records that they were told.”

“That’s not the same thing.”

“It rarely is.”

Mallory crossed the low garden wall. Her boots made almost no sound on the path. The house loomed over her, respectable and sleeping. She stood before the front door, looking at the brass number four fixed to the brick.

For a moment, she did not move.

She remembered Daphne Campbell at nineteen, furious in a training room, refusing to stay down. Daphne at twenty-five, arguing policy with senior officers who mistook kindness for softness. Daphne holding a newborn Hughie and saying, with wonder and terror, that she had never been trusted with anything so breakable.

Mallory lowered the baby gently onto the doorstep, tucking the blanket close against the night air. She placed the envelope beside him, then reconsidered and slid it partly under the blanket, where a careless hand would not miss it.

Hughie sighed in his sleep.

The lightning mark on his forehead glimmered faintly and went dull.

Mallory stood. Her face had returned to stone.

When she came back to the pavement, Butcher had both hands clenched at his sides.

“Just like that,” he said.

“No,” Mallory answered. “Not just like that.”

No one spoke for nearly a minute.

Privet Drive remained quiet. Behind the curtains of number four, Stan Edgar did not come down. Madelyn Stillwell did not open the door. A-Train did not wake. The house accepted the secret on its step without knowing it, and the night closed around them all.

At last Mallory touched the dampening device again. Farther down the street, the lamps brightened by degrees. The security cameras resumed their blind rotations, having recorded nothing useful.

Maeve looked once more at the bundle. “He’ll be famous.”

“Yes.”

“Children will learn his name.”

“Yes.”

“People will make him into whatever they need.”

“Only if we let them reach him too soon.”

Maeve’s gaze stayed on the door. “And when he is old enough?”

“Then we tell him the truth.”

Butcher snorted softly. “Truth has a way of kicking the door in whether you invite it or not.”

Mallory looked at him. “Then perhaps you had better be ready when it does.”

He understood the order beneath the words. His eyes moved to the baby again, and something like a vow settled over his face.

“I’ll be around,” he said.

“Not too close.”

“Didn’t say close.”

“Billy.”

He raised both hands. “Fine. Not close. But if Edgar so much as—”

“You will do nothing that exposes the child.”

Butcher’s smile was thin and dangerous. “Course not.”

Maeve gave him a look. “That means nothing illegal, reckless, explosive, or traceable.”

“Now you’re just taking the joy out of it.”

For the first time all night, Mallory almost smiled. Almost.

The sound of distant sirens drifted over the rooftops, faint and uncertain. Somewhere across Britain, hidden rooms were full of people talking too loudly. Somewhere, operatives were drinking to survival, analysts were denying tears over sensor data, and old enemies were erasing files before dawn. In orbital dark, machines watched for any sign that Homelander’s disappearance was not final.

But on Privet Drive, the most important person in that vast hidden world slept in a gray blanket on a doorstep.

Butcher climbed back onto the flying motorcycle. The anti-gravity vanes whined as they warmed.

“You need transport?” he asked Maeve.

“I’ll walk.”

“To where?”

“Until I stop wanting to break their door.”

Butcher considered this and nodded. “Healthy.”

Mallory stepped away from the curb. “Go. Both of you.”

Maeve lingered another moment. Then she turned and disappeared into the darkness between two houses with the silent certainty of someone who could have crossed rooftops if she wished.

Butcher kicked the motorcycle’s engine. The machine rose a few feet, wobbling as one stabilizer spat sparks.

“Get that repaired,” Mallory said.

“Sentimental value.”

“It is a death trap.”

“That too.”

He looked once more at the doorstep. His expression broke, briefly and completely.

“Happy birthday when it comes, lad,” he murmured, though the birthday was far away and the child could not hear him.

Then the motorcycle climbed, its roar swallowed by the dampeners until it was only a tremor in the air. It vanished into the low clouds, heading north.

Mallory remained alone on the pavement.

She walked to the corner, then stopped and looked back. The bundle was barely visible from the street. A passerby in daylight would see it at once. A milkman would see it. Madelyn Stillwell, opening the door in the morning to complain about bottles, would certainly see it.

Mallory hoped the letter would do enough.

She knew it would not.

“Good luck, Hughie Campbell,” she said quietly.

Then Grace Mallory turned, and between one pool of lamplight and the next, she was gone.

A light wind moved through the hedges of Privet Drive. The houses slept in their tidy rows, smug and unaware beneath a sky full of hidden satellites, grieving heroes, frightened collaborators, and rumors racing faster than aircraft.

On the doorstep of number four, Hughie Campbell shifted in his blanket. One small hand closed around the edge of the envelope tucked beside him. He did not wake. He did not know that his parents were gone. He did not know that the most feared superhuman on Earth had tried to kill him and failed. He did not know that, in secure rooms and off-world channels and academy halls he would not see for years, people were already speaking of the baby with the lightning scar.

He did not know he was famous.

He did not know he had survived.

And all across the hidden defense world, while ordinary Britain slept through official explanations and carefully edited news, operatives raised glasses, bowed heads, opened encrypted memorial files, and whispered the same impossible toast:

“To Hughie Campbell—the boy who survived.”
